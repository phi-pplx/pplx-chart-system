# Jacques Bertin's *Semiology of Graphics*: A Theoretical Research Brief

*Sémiologie Graphique*, 1967. English translations: W.J. Berg (University of Wisconsin Press, 1983); ESRI Press reissue (2011). Page citations refer to the Berg/ESRI translation unless noted.*

---

## Part I: Foundations

### 1. The Thesis

Bertin's central claim is formal and narrow: graphics constitutes a **monosemic sign-system** — a system in which the meaning of each sign is determined prior to, and independently of, the collection of signs in which it appears. This places graphics in the same epistemic family as mathematics, and definitively outside the family of natural language, music, and figurative painting.

The tripartite classification Bertin develops — drawn from his exchange with linguist Christian Metz in response to commentary in *Annales* (1971) — is as follows ([Scribd, Semiology of Graphics](https://www.scribd.com/doc/299457171/Semiology-of-Graphics)):

| System | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Monosemic** | "The meaning of each sign is known prior to observation of the collection of signs. The reading operation takes place *among* the given meanings." | Mathematics, graphics |
| **Polysemic** | "The meaning of the individual signs follows and is deduced from consideration of the collection of signs. Signification becomes subjective and debatable." | Natural language, figurative imagery |
| **Pansemic** | An extreme form of polysemy that "attempts to signify everything and thus no longer signifies anything precise." | Abstract painting |

The consequence is strict: a graphic that requires the viewer to bring external interpretive frameworks — cultural, symbolic, aesthetic — to determine meaning has exited the domain of graphics and entered figurative imagery. Bertin's theory applies only to constructions that remain within the monosemic domain.

Why graphics is monosemic rather than polysemic: because the variables that constitute a graphic (position, size, value, etc.) are mapped, through an explicit legend or construction convention, to predefined data components before the image is read. The reading operation confirms or queries an already-specified correspondence. There is no subjective disambiguation. As Bertin writes: "Graphic representation constitutes one of the basic sign-systems conceived by the human mind for the purposes of storing, understanding, and communicating essential information. As a 'language' for the eye, graphics benefits from the ubiquitous properties of visual perception. As a monosemic system, it forms the rational part of the world of images." (p. 2) ([ESRI 2011 edition](https://paul.zhdk.ch/pluginfile.php/162444/mod_resource/content/3/Jacques%20Bertin%20-%20Semiology%20of%20Graphics_%20Diagrams,%20Networks,%20Maps-Esri%20Press%20(2011).pdf))

Note the structural difference from de Saussure: Bertin's semiology is syntactic, not semantic. He is not concerned with what signs *mean* in a cultural sense; he is concerned with the formal rules by which signs are correctly constructed and read. This alignment with Peirce rather than Saussure is explicit in later reception ([mappingasprocess.net](https://www.mappingasprocess.net/blog/2021/8/20/some-thoughts-on-jacques-bertins-cartographic-semiology)).

### 2. Definition of Graphic Representation

**Precise definition** (p. 2): "Graphic representation is the transcription, into the graphic sign-system, of 'information' known through the intermediary of any given sign-system." ([ESRI 2011](https://paul.zhdk.ch/pluginfile.php/162444/mod_resource/content/3/Jacques%20Bertin%20-%20Semiology%20of%20Graphics_%20Diagrams,%20Networks,%20Maps-Esri%20Press%20(2011).pdf))

Graphics is further defined by its domain: "Within the boundaries of graphics fall the fields of networks, diagrams and maps." (p. 2) This excludes photography, figurative illustration, painting, film, and text, even when these appear in the same document as a graphic.

**The three functions** (pp. 4–5):

1. **Recording** (inventory drawings): Creating a storage mechanism that "avoids the effort of memorization." A recording graphic must be comprehensive; it need not be memorizable.
2. **Communicating** (simplified drawings / messages): Creating a memorizable image that "will inscribe the information in the viewer's mind." A communicating graphic must be memorizable; it may be noncomprehensive.
3. **Processing** (graphics used for processing): Furnishing drawings "which permit a simplification and its justification." A processing graphic must be both memorizable (for comparisons) and comprehensive (for choices). This is the most formally significant function — the reorderable matrix belongs here.

**Figurative vs. non-figurative graphics**: Figurative graphics use shape to represent the physical appearance of objects (maps of coastlines, technical illustrations of machinery). Non-figurative graphics use marks whose shape carries no referential analogy to what is represented — a bar chart, a scatter plot. Bertin's theory applies primarily to non-figurative graphics, where the encoding is purely conventional. In figurative graphics, shape may carry referential meaning beyond the graphic system, introducing polysemy at that variable ([fineartdrawinglca.blogspot.com](http://fineartdrawinglca.blogspot.com/2023/06/bertin-and-organising-information.html)).

### 3. The Graphic System

The graphic system consists of two classes of components ([Scribd, Semiology of Graphics](https://www.scribd.com/document/412565176/Semiology-of-Graphics-Jacques-Bertin)):

**The plane** (the two planar dimensions, *les deux dimensions du plan*): The x-axis and y-axis of any flat surface. These are the primary encoding dimensions. A mark's position on the plane is not a retinal variable — it is the plane itself, and it has unique properties: it is simultaneously selective, associative, ordered, and quantitative at every level. The plane is homogeneous; it possesses two dimensions; and it "has the properties of: selectivity, associativity, order, and quantity" (p. 44). No retinal variable matches the plane for encoding capacity.

**The retinal variables** (les variables rétiniennes): Everything that can vary at a fixed point on the plane without changing that point's position. Bertin identifies six: SIZE, VALUE, TEXTURE, COLOR, ORIENTATION, SHAPE. Together with the two planar dimensions, these constitute the **eight visual variables** of the graphic system.

The term "retinal" is deliberate and technical: these variables act directly on the retina without requiring learned symbolic associations. They are pre-symbolic in this sense — the perception of "larger" or "darker" does not require cultural training in the way that reading text does ([Roth 2015, UW-Madison Geography](https://geography.wisc.edu/cartography/projects/publications/Roth_2015_EG.pdf)).

## Part II: The Seven (Eight) Visual Variables — Exhaustive

### Preliminary Definitions

Before cataloguing the variables, Bertin establishes the analytical vocabulary:

- **Level of organization**: The highest perceptual property a variable possesses. A variable with quantitative perception also possesses ordered, selective, and associative perception (though not always — size is dissociative, not associative). The four levels, from lowest to highest: **selective** (≠), **associative** (≡), **ordered** (O), **quantitative** (Q).
- **Length** (*longueur*): "The number of divisions identifiable in a given component" (p. 33). Each variable has a practical upper limit of discriminable steps before confusion occurs.
- **Implantation**: The geometric form of the mark — point, line, or zone (area). A variable's properties differ depending on which implantation it modifies.

### 2.1 The Two Planar Dimensions (x, y)

**Definition**: The two orthogonal dimensions of the plane. Any mark's position on the plane constitutes a simultaneous encoding on both dimensions. "A visible mark can vary in position on a sheet of paper" (p. 44). The plane possesses all four perceptual properties at once — it is the richest encoding channel in the system.

**Length**: In principle unlimited; bounded in practice by the resolution of the rendering surface and the minimum discriminable distance between marks (Bertin specifies 2/10 mm as the minimum mark diameter).

- **Selective**: Yes. The viewer can isolate all marks at a given x or y position.
- **Associative**: Yes. Marks can be grouped regardless of their planar positions (when another variable is used for grouping).
- **Ordered**: Yes. Position naturally implies an ordering from low to high or left to right.
- **Quantitative**: Yes. The distance between two positions can be estimated as a ratio.

**Recommended application**: Every component that requires the highest encoding fidelity should be assigned to the planar dimensions first. Quantitative components (ratio or interval data) should preferably occupy the plane. Geographic components *must* occupy the plane in a map.

---

### 2.2 Size (*Taille*)

**Definition** (p. 71): "It is a variation in the dimensions of the mark (area) which constitutes the perceptual stimulus for size variation." For points, size is the area of the symbol. For lines, size is line weight (width). Size does not apply in the same sense to zones (areas already fill a region).

**Length** (discriminable steps): 4–5 for points; 4 for lines (per Bertin's table, p. 96). The channel saturates quickly because area estimation is imprecise beyond a small number of steps.

- **Selective**: Yes. Large marks stand apart from small marks pre-attentively.
- **Associative**: No. Size is **dissociative**: variation in size dominates perception such that groups defined by other variables become harder to perceive when size varies. "A dissociative variable causes the visibility of the signs to vary" (Bertin, cited in [Axis Maps](https://www.axismaps.com/guide/visual-variables)).
- **Ordered**: Yes. Larger means more; smaller means less. The ordering is immediate and universal.
- **Quantitative**: Yes — with qualification. "We must remember that quantitative perception represents an accurate approximation but not a precise measurement" (Bertin, cited in [Axis Maps](https://www.axismaps.com/guide/visual-variables)).

**Recommended application**: Quantitative or ordered components. The *only* retinal variable Bertin classifies as quantitative. Appropriate for encoding population, magnitude, or any data component where proportionality between steps is perceptually useful. Not appropriate for nominal (categorical) data because it implies an order and a ratio that do not exist.

---

### 2.3 Value (*Valeur*)

**Definition** (p. 73): "Value variation is the continuous progression which the eye perceives in a series of grays ranging from black to white."

Value is entirely distinct from color (hue) in Bertin's system. A series of grays with no hue variation is value. A series of distinct hues at identical lightness levels is color. The confusion between these two variables is, for Bertin, one of the most common construction errors in information graphics.

**Length** (discriminable steps): 3–5 for points; 4 for lines; 6–7 for zones (areas).

- **Selective**: Yes. Darker regions stand apart from lighter regions pre-attentively.
- **Associative**: No. Value is **dissociative**: "Value and Size change the visibility of the symbols, so that a variation of visibility makes it impossible or very hard to ignore that variation" ([ICACI proceedings](https://icaci.org/files/documents/ICC_proceedings/ICC2011/Oral%20Presentations%20PDF/A4-Jacques%20Bertin%20and%20graphic%20semiology%202/CO-054.pdf)).
- **Ordered**: Yes. The gray scale is universally perceived as an ordered sequence (darker = more, lighter = less).
- **Quantitative**: No. Bertin explicitly excludes value from the quantitative class; the steps cannot be reliably estimated as ratios.

**Recommended application**: Ordered or quantitative data components, particularly when encoded over zones (choropleth maps, heatmaps). Bertin is explicit that ordered data should use value (lightness) rather than hue — hue has no inherent order.

---

### 2.4 Texture (*Grain*)

**Definition** (p. 79): "At a given value, the TEXTURE is the number of separable marks contained in a unitary area." Texture is the density of sub-marks within a region. A fine texture has many small marks per unit area; a coarse texture has few large marks per unit area.

The French term *grain* is literally "grain" (as in the grain of wood or photographic film). Berg's 1983 translation uses "texture"; some scholars prefer "granularity" as a more precise rendering ([Wikipedia: Visual variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_variable)).

**Length** (discriminable steps): 2–3 for points; 3–4 for lines; 4–5 for zones.

- **Selective**: Yes.
- **Associative**: Yes. Unlike size and value, texture does not dominate perception to the degree that it prevents grouping by other variables.
- **Ordered**: Yes (marginally). Fine-to-coarse is perceptible as a sequence, though MacEachren (1995) later argued texture is only weakly ordered in practice ([Roth 2015](https://geography.wisc.edu/cartography/projects/publications/Roth_2015_EG.pdf)).
- **Quantitative**: No.

**Recommended application**: Ordered nominal data (when categories have a weak rank), or as a secondary ordered variable alongside value. In practice, texture has limited utility in screen-rendered graphics due to the regularization of sub-pixel rendering.

---

### 2.5 Color (*Couleur*)

**Definition** (p. 85): "COLOR variation is simply the perceptible difference which can be perceived between uniform areas having the same value." This is the critical clause: color in Bertin's system means **hue only**, at equal value (lightness). Two hues (red and blue) at different lightness levels differ in *both* color and value; Bertin requires these to be separated analytically.

**Length** (discriminable steps): 7 — consistently applied across point, line, and zone implantations. This is the categorical capacity of hue: the eye can distinguish approximately seven distinct hue categories before confusion occurs.

Bertin's warning on color (p. 222, *Graphics and Graphic Information-Processing*, 1981): "I am against color when it masks incompetence; when it allows the superimposition of characteristics to the point of absurdity; when people believe it capable of representing ordered data." ([Wikiquote: Jacques Bertin](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jacques_Bertin))

- **Selective**: Yes. Distinct hues are isolable pre-attentively.
- **Associative**: Yes. Marks of the same hue are perceptually grouped, and hue variation does not dominate the visibility of marks the way size or value does.
- **Ordered**: **No.** This is Bertin's most operationally important statement about color. There is no perceptual sequence running from red through green to blue. Any attempt to use hue to encode an ordered or quantitative component is a construction error. (Hue has a cyclical order — the color wheel — but this is not the linear order required for ordinal or quantitative data.)
- **Quantitative**: **No.**

**Recommended application**: Nominal (categorical) data components exclusively. Color encodes qualitative difference — that category A is different from category B — without implying that A is more or less than B. Seven is the maximum number of categories before hue discrimination degrades.

---

### 2.6 Orientation (*Orientation*)

**Definition** (p. 93): "It is the difference in angle between fields created by several parallel signs which constitutes the perceptible 'stimulus' for orientation variation."

Orientation applies to marks that have a discernible axis: hatching patterns, dashes, arrow-like symbols, angled glyphs. A circular dot has no orientation; a rectangle does.

**Length** (discriminable steps): 4 for points; 2 for lines; 4 for zones.

- **Selective**: Yes for points and lines. **No for zones**: when orientation is applied to an area fill (e.g., a polygon filled with diagonal hatching), it is not pre-attentively selective — the viewer cannot quickly isolate "all areas with 45° hatching" from among multiple orientations ([Roth 2015](https://geography.wisc.edu/cartography/projects/publications/Roth_2015_EG.pdf)).
- **Associative**: Yes. Marks with the same orientation group perceptually.
- **Ordered**: **No** (except cyclically). There is no "more orientation" or "less orientation" in a linear sense. Orientation has a cyclical structure (0° = 180° = 360°) but not a linear one.
- **Quantitative**: No.

**Recommended application**: Nominal data. Orientation effectively distinguishes categories (e.g., type of land use on a map rendered with different hatching patterns). It should not be used for ordered or quantitative components. Its utility diminishes for zone implantation.

---

### 2.7 Shape (*Forme*)

**Definition** (pp. 95–96): "It is the element of 'similarity' recognized in the shape which constitutes the stimulus for this variable." Shape is the silhouette or outline of a mark, independent of its size.

Shape has theoretically unlimited length — the repertoire of distinguishable shapes is infinite. This is simultaneously its strength and its diagnostic weakness.

**Length**: Infinite (for points and lines); but not selective.

- **Selective**: **No.** This is Bertin's most unqualified negative assessment. Shape cannot be used to pre-attentively isolate a category from a complex field. "Bertin contends that shape (for points, lines and areas) and orientation (only when applied to areas) are not selective" ([geo.uzh.ch paper](https://www.geo.uzh.ch/~sara/pubs/garlandini_fabs09.pdf)).
- **Associative**: Yes. Marks of the same shape can be grouped — but this grouping requires sequential scanning, not pre-attentive perception.
- **Ordered**: No.
- **Quantitative**: No.

**Recommended application**: Nominal data for small component lengths (few categories). Because shape is not selective, it is inappropriate for any display where the viewer needs to rapidly identify a category within a complex field. It is most effective in maps or diagrams with a limited number of mark types, and where a legend can be consulted.

## Part III: The Three Levels of Reading

Bertin formalizes three levels at which a viewer can question a graphic ([ESRI 2011](https://paul.zhdk.ch/pluginfile.php/162444/mod_resource/content/3/Jacques%20Bertin%20-%20Semiology%20of%20Graphics_%20Diagrams,%20Networks,%20Maps-Esri%20Press%20(2011).pdf); [fineartdrawinglca.blogspot.com](http://fineartdrawinglca.blogspot.com/2023/06/bertin-and-organising-information.html)):

| Level | Definition | Example Question | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Elementary** | A question introduced by a single element of a component | "On a given date, what is the price of stock X?" | A single correspondence |
| **Intermediate** | A question introduced by a group of elements in a component | "In the first three days, what was the trend of the price?" | A reduction of component length |
| **Overall (global)** | A question introduced by the whole component | "During the entire period, what was the trend of the price?" | A single ordered relationship among all components |

The operational test for graphic quality follows directly: "A construction is more efficient if it requires a shorter observation time to obtain a correct and complete answer to a given question." (p. 139) ([Wikiquote: Jacques Bertin](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jacques_Bertin)) The definition of *information* in this framework is also precise: "Information is the reply to a question." (p. 11)

A well-constructed graphic answers the question at the **overall level** in a single instant of visual perception — without sequential scanning, without consulting the legend for every element, and without reconstructing the answer mentally from elementary lookups. A graphic that can only be read at the elementary or intermediate level is, by Bertin's definition, an inefficient construction.

The three levels of reading also correspond to the three basic questions Bertin identifies in a 2003 interview ([Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jacques_Bertin)):
1. What are the X, Y, Z components? (What is this about?)
2. What are the groups in X and Y that Z constructs? (What patterns exist?)
3. What are the exceptions?

A graphic that requires a legend to answer question 1 has failed at the most basic level of construction.

## Part IV: Constructing the Image

### 4.1 The Concept of "Image"

Bertin distinguishes two units of visual experience ([countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf)):

- **Image** (p. 142): "The meaningful visual form perceptible in the minimum instant of vision will be called the IMAGE." It corresponds to what Gestalt psychology calls *Gestalt* — a form perceived as a whole, not assembled from parts. The image is the temporal unit of meaningful visual perception.

- **Figure** (p. 142): "The image... must not be confused with the FIGURE, which is the apparent and illusory unit defined by the sheet of paper, by a linear frame or by a geographic border." A figure is a physical container; an image is a perceptual event.

The two-variable constraint that makes an image possible: "All information with three components or less can be represented as a single image." (p. 148) More precisely: the two planar dimensions plus one retinal variable = three components = the maximum that can be read in a single instant ([countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf)). Beyond three actively encoded components, the viewer cannot perceive the construction in a single instant; instead, they must construct an answer sequentially in memory — producing a **figuration** rather than an image.

"In any construction not respecting these rules... the entire set of correspondences could only be constructed in the memory of the reader, by the addition of the various images perceived in succession. We will call these graphic constructions FIGURATIONS." (pp. 11–12)

---

### 4.2 The Construction Process

Bertin specifies a formal procedure for graphic construction (pp. 16–52) ([countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf)):

1. **Analyze the information.** Identify the *invariant* (the central notion common to all correspondences — what the graphic is about) and the *components* (the variational concepts — what differs from case to case).

2. **Determine component count.** How many distinct variational concepts exist in the dataset?

3. **Determine component length.** For each component, how many divisions or categories exist?

4. **Determine level of organization.** Assign each component to its appropriate level:
   - **Qualitative** (≠): Simple differentiation — categories differ but have no inherent rank.
   - **Ordered** (O): Elements can be ranked in a universal manner.
   - **Quantitative** (Q): Elements are specifiable by a countable unit permitting ratio comparisons.

5. **Determine implantation.** Assign each information unit to a geometric form: point, line, or zone. The choice of implantation is constrained by the data structure — not by aesthetics.

6. **Determine imposition.** Choose the graphic group: diagram, network, map, or symbol.

7. **Elevate with retinal variables.** Assign retinal variables to remaining components. The assignment must respect the level of organization: a quantitative component should receive a quantitative-capable variable (planar dimensions or size); an ordered component should receive an ordered variable (value, texture); a nominal component should receive an associative variable (color, orientation, shape).

8. **Test against the three levels of reading.** Can the overall question be answered in a single glance? If not, the construction is a figuration — revise the variable assignments and iterate.

---

### 4.3 Common Construction Errors

Bertin enumerates specific construction errors ([countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf)):

1. **The QS² error** (p. 45): Extending a value or color encoding a quantity (Q) over an entire zone (S). Because the zone's area is already proportional to S, the eye reads the quantity as squared (Q × S). This error is ubiquitous in choropleth maps where raw counts are used instead of rates.

2. **Level mismatch** (p. 34, 48): Using a visual variable whose level of organization is lower than the data component's level. The canonical case: using **color (hue)** — which is selective and associative but not ordered — to represent an **ordered** component (e.g., temperature, population density). This is the most common violation in data visualization practice.

3. **Informational lacunae** (p. 46): Leaving empty spaces in a signifying field. "In a signifying space absence of signs signifies absence of phenomena." An empty cell in a matrix does not mean "no data"; it signifies that the corresponding data value is zero or null. If this is not the intended meaning, the construction is false.

4. **Non-ordered value** (p. 76, 86): Using gray levels or color shades in an order that does not correspond to the numerical order of the data. Value must always be monotonically ordered with respect to the quantity it represents.

5. **Color spectral confusion** (p. 87, 89): Combining "warm" and "cool" scales in a single ordered series. The eye groups the dark ends of the spectrum together (dark red and dark blue both recede) rather than following the intended progression. This produces a perceptual reversal in the middle of the scale.

6. **Aesthetic distraction** (p. 46): Varying a visual variable for decorative purposes that correspond to no component of the data. "Show data variation, not design variation" — a principle Bertin states formally and Tufte later restates rhetorically (see Part VIII).

## Part V: Reorderable Matrices

Bertin's second major theoretical contribution, formalized in *La Graphique et le Traitement Graphique de l'Information* (1977, translated 1981), is the **reorderable matrix** ([INRIA HAL paper](https://inria.hal.science/hal-01786606/file/bertin.pdf)).

**The matrix as a graphic**: When both dimensions of a two-dimensional data table are *reorderable* (i.e., neither dimension has a fixed natural order that must be preserved, such as time), the appropriate visualization is a matrix in which cell values are encoded visually — via size, value, or texture. This visual matrix is a graphic construction in Bertin's full sense: it is subject to the same visual variable logic as any diagram or map.

**The criterion for reorderability**: A dimension is reorderable if its elements can be permuted without violating the meaning of the data. Time series are not reorderable (January must precede February). Geographic coordinates are not reorderable in a map context. But categories — types of industry, species, demographic groups — typically are reorderable.

"For Bertin, the matrix was the most general and clearly the most important visual construction." ([INRIA HAL paper](https://inria.hal.science/hal-01786606/file/bertin.pdf))

**The permutation procedure**: Bertin's manual method — what he called "glue and scissors" — proceeds as follows ([INRIA HAL paper](https://inria.hal.science/hal-01786606/file/bertin.pdf)):

1. Draw or print the matrix.
2. Photocopy it.
3. Cut the copy into horizontal strips (rows).
4. Manually reorder the strips to maximize visual similarity between adjacent rows (rows with similar patterns of dark and light cells should be adjacent).
5. Glue into the found order.
6. Repeat for columns.
7. Photocopy the result.
8. Identify and name emergent groups.

The operational criterion for choosing the next permutation: move the row or column that, when placed adjacent to its most similar neighbor, reduces the total boundary between differently-valued regions. The goal is to make all cells of similar value form contiguous blocks — ideally, a small number of rectangular regions covering the matrix.

**The image that emerges**: A well-ordered matrix becomes an image in Bertin's sense: the groupings of rows and columns are visible in a single glance, without sequential reading. Bertin: "This is a fundamental point, because it is the internal mobility of the image which characterizes modern graphics. A graphic is no longer 'drawn' once and for all; it is 'constructed' and reconstructed (manipulated) until all the relationships which lie within it have been perceived." (*Graphics and Graphic Information-Processing*, 1981) ([INRIA HAL paper](https://inria.hal.science/hal-01786606/file/bertin.pdf); [historyofinformation.com](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?id=3361))

**The conceptual distinction from charts**: A static bar chart or line chart is a *fixed* construction — drawn once, read once, with no internal mobility. The reorderable matrix is a *living image*, a research instrument. The distinction Bertin draws is not aesthetic but functional: "The graphic is no longer only the 'representation' of a final simplification, it is a point of departure for the discovery of these simplifications and the means for their justification." (p. 4) This reframes the graphic from a communication artifact (post-hoc representation of a known result) to a processing instrument (pre-hoc exploration of an unknown structure).

Bertin explicitly criticized automated reordering algorithms as producing "unsatisfactory" output and argued that the analyst (decision-maker) must remain directly involved: "The best graphic operations are those carried out by the decision-maker himself." (*La Graphique*, 1981) ([INRIA HAL paper](https://inria.hal.science/hal-01786606/file/bertin.pdf))

The reorderable matrix is the conceptual ancestor of: clustering heatmaps (hierarchical clustering applied to rows and columns), scagnostics (algebraic ordering of scatterplot matrices), and all interactive sorting/filtering in modern analytics dashboards.

## Part VI: Maps and Networks

### 6.1 Taxonomy of Graphic Groups

Bertin classifies graphic constructions into four **groups** (*impositions*), distinguished by the type of correspondence established ([countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf)):

| Group | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Diagram** | Correspondences established between all divisions of one component and all divisions of another component (Cartesian mapping). |
| **Network** | Correspondences established among all divisions of *the same* component (within-component relations). |
| **Map** | Correspondences among all elements of a geographic component, arranged according to geographic order (the plane encodes geographic space). |
| **Symbol** | Correspondence between a single element and the reader, exterior to the graphic system (relies on figurative analogy or acquired convention). |

The distinction between diagram and network is formally significant: a diagram maps *between* two different components; a network maps *within* a single component. A road network is a network (it shows connections among places — one component). A scatter plot is a diagram (it maps values on one variable against values on another).

### 6.2 Networks and Their Variables

In a network, the **line** is the primary mark. Bertin identifies lines as subject to all retinal variables except those that presuppose a fixed orientation (orientation is applicable to lines only in the sense of the direction of the line segment, not an imposed angle fill). For line marks ([countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf)):

| Variable | Point capacity | **Line capacity** | Zone capacity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Size | 4–5 | **4** | — |
| Value | 3–5 | **4** | 6–7 |
| Texture | 2–3 | **3–4** | 4–5 |
| Color | 7 | **7** | 7 |
| Orientation | 4 | **2** | 4 |
| Shape | ∞ (not selective) | **∞ (not selective)** | ∞ (not selective) |

A line can thus encode: its path (positional/geographic information), **size** (weight/width — encoding quantity or hierarchy), **value** (darkness — encoding order), **color** (hue — encoding category), and **texture** (dashing pattern — encoding ordered category or type). Bertin notes that a line's *direction* (its path through the plane) is not a retinal variable — it is a property of the planar encoding.

**Networks and legibility**: "Networks cease to be a means of discovery when elements are numerous, as the figure becomes complex, illegible, and untransformable." (p. 129, *La Graphique*, 1981) ([Wikiquote: Jacques Bertin](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jacques_Bertin)) This is Bertin's critique of what is now called "hairball" network visualization — a figuration, not an image.

### 6.3 Map Types and Visual Variables on Zones

Maps assign geographic order to the planar dimensions — the x and y axes are reserved for longitude and latitude (or their projection equivalents). This means all thematic information must be encoded in retinal variables applied to points, lines, or zones.

- **Point implantation on maps**: Cities, events, samples. Size encodes quantity; color encodes category; shape encodes type.
- **Line implantation on maps**: Roads, rivers, boundaries. Size (weight) encodes importance or volume; color encodes category.
- **Zone implantation on maps**: Regions, polygons, administrative areas. Value is the primary variable for ordered/quantitative data (choropleth maps); color for nominal data.

The critical constraint: the plane is occupied by geography, so it cannot simultaneously encode a data component. All data encoding must therefore use retinal variables — with the attendant reduction in encoding capacity compared to a diagram where both planar dimensions are free.

## Part VII: Where Bertin Disagrees with Later Canon

### 7.1 Bertin vs. Cleveland (1984/1985)

Cleveland and McGill's 1984 paper "Graphical Perception: Theory, Experimentation, and Application" and Cleveland's 1985 book *The Elements of Graphing Data* establish a **ranked hierarchy** of elementary perceptual tasks, from most to least accurate ([Priceonomics: Cleveland](https://priceonomics.com/how-william-cleveland-turned-data-visualization/)):

1. Position along a common scale
2. Position along non-aligned, identical scales
3. Length, direction, angle
4. Area
5. Volume, curvature
6. Shading, color saturation

The disagreement with Bertin is structural, not empirical. Cleveland's hierarchy is a **universal ranking of precision** for quantitative judgment tasks: given any data to encode, choose the encoding from the top of the hierarchy because it will be decoded with the greatest accuracy.

Bertin's framework is **non-hierarchical and type-matched**: variables are not ranked against each other but matched to data types. Size is appropriate for quantitative data; color is appropriate for nominal data. There is no single ranking because the question "which variable is best?" is only answerable once the data type is specified. Under Bertin's framework, using position on a common scale (Cleveland's #1) to encode nominal category data is not optimal — it implies a spatial ordering that the data do not possess. Using color hue (Cleveland's #6 proxy) for nominal categories is the correct Bertin choice, even though hue is less precise.

The genuine tension: Cleveland and Bertin agree that quantitative data should use position and size (both frameworks align here). They disagree about what "best" means when the data are nominal or ordinal. Cleveland's framework implicitly assumes quantitative judgment as the universal goal. Bertin's framework treats the matching of variable to data type as the primary criterion, with precision as secondary.

### 7.2 Bertin vs. Tufte

Edward Tufte's *The Visual Display of Quantitative Information* (1983) and subsequent books operate at a different layer of the problem. Tufte articulates a set of *post-hoc judgments* about existing visualizations — principles for evaluating whether a constructed graphic is good or bad. His core principles (data-ink maximization, chartjunk elimination, lie factor, data density) are evaluative criteria applied after construction.

Bertin's framework is *a priori* and *constructive*: it specifies the rules for *building* a graphic before any mark is placed. The question is not "is this too much ink?" but "which variable should encode this component, and why?"

The operational difference: Tufte's "erase non-data-ink" is a post-hoc editing rule with no specific quantitative threshold. Bertin's "color encodes only nominal components" is a pre-construction specification that is either satisfied or violated — unambiguously. As critics note: "It is unclear how a data-ink ratio can be accurately calculated in practice, and Tufte makes estimations rather than numerical calculations" ([semanticscholar.org](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/1463/e8f5e94152888128e75a84c6e58cad41ae1b.pdf)). Bertin's criteria carry no such ambiguity.

Tufte and Bertin do agree on some conclusions — both condemn encoding ordered data with hue alone (Bertin by formal rule; Tufte by rhetorical case study) — but they arrive at agreement through different means. Tufte's framework is more accessible and more influential in design culture; Bertin's is more rigorous and more falsifiable.

Note: Bertin himself condemned multiple pie charts as "completely useless" (p. 111, *La Graphique*), a conclusion Tufte reaches independently through aesthetic and ethical arguments ([dvia.samizdat.co](https://dvia.samizdat.co/2019/work/gitacoco/0.research/Zhibang_TheVisualDisplay.pdf)).

### 7.3 Bertin vs. Few and Dashboard Culture

Stephen Few's work on dashboard design inherits from both Tufte (aesthetic minimalism) and Cleveland (perceptual hierarchy), but does not engage with Bertin's image constraint directly.

The Bertin-implied critique of dashboard culture is structural: a standard information dashboard encodes ten to thirty independent data components simultaneously, each using multiple retinal variables. Each panel may be a well-constructed image; the dashboard as a whole is a figuration — it cannot be read at the overall level in a single instant. The viewer must move sequentially through panels, constructing an integrated answer in working memory.

Bertin: "In any construction not respecting these rules... the entire set of correspondences could only be constructed in the memory of the reader, by the addition of the various images perceived in succession." (pp. 11–12) The dashboard does not fail because it has too much ink; it fails because it exceeds the three-component image constraint. Bertin's critique is at the level of *architecture*, not aesthetics.

The image constraint also implies: a well-designed dashboard is not a single image — it is a set of images, each answering one question. The failure mode is treating the dashboard *as if* it were a single image, which leads to overcrowding each panel in an attempt to add components beyond the three-component limit.

## Part VIII: Why Bertin Is More Rigorous Than Tufte

The comparison requires precision: "more rigorous" means that Bertin's framework is more formally specified, more falsifiable, and operates at the level of theory rather than rhetoric or case-study argument.

**Bertin's framework is a theory with definitions, propositions, and procedures**. It defines its terms formally (monosemic, image, implantation, level of organization, length). It states propositions that are checkable against any specific graphic (a color variable encoding ordered data is a construction error — full stop). It provides a construction procedure that generates a unique output for a given dataset and question.

**Tufte's framework is a body of criticism with heuristics and case studies**. Data-ink ratio is defined (data-ink / total ink), but the calculation requires subjective judgments about which ink is "data-ink" in any particular case. Chartjunk is defined as "interior decoration of graphics" that "generates a lot of ink that does not tell the viewer anything new" — but what counts as telling the viewer something new is contestable. Researchers testing Tufte's claims have found that "some non-data-ink and redundant data-ink... may decrease accuracy and increase response time" — but also that "chartjunk" can increase memorability ([performancemagazine.org](https://www.performancemagazine.org/data-ink-ratio-minimalism-data-visualization/); [arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.22051v1)). Tufte's principles have been "characterized as having a basis in Tufte's design intuitions and lacking experimental validation" ([semanticscholar.org](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/1463/e8f5e94152888128e75a84c6e58cad41ae1b.pdf)).

A concrete example: given a choropleth map using hue to encode population density (an ordered variable).
- Bertin's verdict: **construction error** — ordered data must use an ordered variable (value/lightness), not a nominal-only variable (hue). The verdict requires no subjective judgment.
- Tufte's verdict: depends on whether the color choices create what he considers "chartjunk" — a judgment that requires aesthetic interpretation.

Bertin's framework was identified alongside Tukey's *Exploratory Data Analysis* and Wilkinson's *Grammar of Graphics* as one of the seminal theoretical contributions to modern data visualization ([arxiv.org Critical Reflection](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.22051v1)). Nicolas Kruchten describes *Semiology of Graphics* as providing "the formalized semiological notion that visualization involves a sign-system wherein visual variables (signifiers) are mapped onto data variables (signifieds)" — the foundational structure from which ggplot2, Vega-Lite, and Tableau's encoding model all derive ([nicolas.kruchten.com](https://nicolas.kruchten.com/semiology_of_graphics/)).

The summary: Bertin writes a *theory*; Tufte writes *criticism*. Both are valuable; they are not comparable in rigor because they are not attempting the same thing.

## Part IX: Modern Application to Technical Diagrams

### 9.1 What Bertin Tells You About Flow Diagrams

In Bertin's taxonomy, a flow diagram is a **network** — correspondences among elements of the same component (nodes). The line is the primary mark. Lines in a flow diagram carry two types of information by construction: **connectivity** (which nodes are connected, encoded in the path itself) and potentially **data attributes** (weight, type, direction, state).

Bertin's framework licenses the following encodings on lines in a technical flow diagram:

- **Line weight (size)**: Encodes quantity or hierarchy. An ordered or quantitative component. Capacity: 4 discriminable steps.
- **Line value (lightness)**: Encodes order. An ordered component. Capacity: 4 discriminable steps.
- **Line color (hue)**: Encodes category. A nominal component only. Capacity: 7 discriminable categories.
- **Line texture (dashing)**: Encodes type or order. Ordered nominal. Capacity: 3–4 discriminable steps.

The constraint: only three of these retinal variables can be active simultaneously (plus the spatial path) without the diagram becoming a figuration. A flow diagram that encodes *category* (color), *weight* (size), *state* (dashing), *direction* (arrowheads), and *priority* (value) on the same lines has six active components and cannot be read as an image.

Furthermore: direction (arrowheads) is a symbol, not a retinal variable in Bertin's system — it relies on a learned convention. When arrowheads and multiple retinal variables are combined on lines, the constructive rule requires that the total number of actively encoded data components remain at three.

### 9.2 What Bertin Tells You About Icons and Pictograms

Icons and pictograms in a technical diagram are Bertin's **symbols** — they establish correspondences through figurative analogy (a server icon looks like a server) or acquired convention (a lock icon means authentication). Symbols sit outside the retinal variable system: their shape encodes meaning through reference, not through the formal variable rules.

The diagnostic question for any icon in a technical diagram: is the icon's shape encoding a **data component** (in which case it is using shape as a retinal variable and has the limitations of shape — not selective, unlimited length, nominal only), or is it encoding **the invariant** (the type of entity — server, database, user), which is not a variable but a fixed label?

If shape is encoding entity type (invariant), it is functioning as a symbol or legend key, not as a data-encoding variable, and is outside Bertin's retinal variable analysis. It carries no data and should not be treated as a variable assignment.

If shape is varying *within* a category to encode data — e.g., using different icon shapes to indicate different states of the same entity type — it is using shape as a retinal variable: selective = No, associative = Yes, ordered = No, quantitative = No. Valid only for nominal distinctions; not pre-attentively distinguishable in a complex field.

### 9.3 What Bertin Tells You About Color in Technical Diagrams

The operational rule is stark and specific: **color (hue) is neither ordered nor quantitative**. Any encoding of a severity level, priority rank, completion percentage, or similar ordered quantity using hue alone is a level-mismatch construction error by Bertin's formal definition.

Correct encodings for ordered data in technical diagrams:
- **Value (lightness/darkness)**: The correct Bertin variable for ordinal data. Dark = high severity, light = low severity. Capacity: 4–7 discriminable steps depending on implantation.
- **Size**: For quantitative components where proportional estimation is useful.

Color (hue) is correct for: entity type (node category), data source, domain, team ownership — any genuinely nominal component with no inherent ordering.

The common violation in technical diagram systems: using a red-yellow-green hue scale for severity or status. This is a level mismatch *and* a spectral confusion error — the "dark" ends of the red and green scales are perceptually similar, producing a discontinuity in the middle of an intended progression. The Bertin-compliant alternative: a single-hue value scale (light to dark in a chosen hue), or a diverging value scale with a neutral midpoint.

### 9.4 What Bertin Tells You About Multi-Variable Encoding

The three-component image constraint is not a guideline — it is the formal boundary condition between an image and a figuration. "All information with three components or less can be represented as a single image" (p. 148). Beyond three, the construction is a figuration requiring sequential reading.

For a technical diagram system:

- **Two planar dimensions** are committed to layout (spatial positioning of nodes, topology of connections).
- **One retinal variable** remains available for a data component in a single-image construction.
- If a second retinal variable is needed, the viewer must parse two overlapping layers — one image per layer. The construction remains efficient only if the questions these two layers answer are separable (can the viewer attend to one variable at a time?).
- A third retinal variable active simultaneously on the same marks is the empirical threshold beyond which most viewers report confusion, legend dependence, and sequential reading ([jonathansoma.com](https://jonathansoma.com/lede/storytelling/classes/dealing-with-data/visual-encoding/)).

The implication for component prioritization: determine the one question the diagram must answer at the overall level. Assign the most capable variable to it (planar dimensions if possible; size or value if not). Treat all other variables as secondary, answerable only at the intermediate or elementary level with legend assistance.

## Appendix A: Bertin Audit Framework

A set of concrete, testable criteria for auditing any technical diagram against Bertin's formal framework.

| # | Property | Test | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Level matching: color (hue)** | Identify every retinal variable assigned to color (hue). Verify the encoded data component is nominal (no inherent order). If the component is ordered (severity, priority, status), the assignment is a construction error. | Bertin 1967/1983, pp. 85–91; [Axis Maps](https://www.axismaps.com/guide/visual-variables) |
| 2 | **Level matching: size** | Identify every retinal variable assigned to size (node area, line weight). Verify the encoded component is either ordered or quantitative. If the component is nominal (category, type), the assignment implies false order. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 71 |
| 3 | **Level matching: value** | Identify every lightness/darkness gradient. Verify the encoded component is ordered. If the component is nominal, the assignment implies false rank. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 73 |
| 4 | **Shape selectivity** | Count the number of distinct shapes used to encode a data component. Ask: can a viewer pre-attentively isolate all marks of one shape in a complex field (>15 marks)? If not, shape is encoding at nominal level without the pre-attentive selectivity required for rapid reading. | Bertin 1967/1983, pp. 95–96; [geo.uzh.ch](https://www.geo.uzh.ch/~sara/pubs/garlandini_fabs09.pdf) |
| 5 | **Component count per diagram** | Count the number of *active* retinal variables in a single diagram (not counting the planar dimensions). If the count exceeds 1 (for a single-image target) or 2 (for a dual-layer target), test whether the overall question can be answered in a single glance without consulting the legend. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 148; [jonathansoma.com](https://jonathansoma.com/lede/storytelling/classes/dealing-with-data/visual-encoding/) |
| 6 | **Planar dimension assignment** | Verify that the most information-dense component (highest level of organization) is assigned to the planar dimensions. If a quantitative component is assigned to a retinal variable while the plane encodes nominal layout, document the encoding loss. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 44 |
| 7 | **Ordered color scale test** | For any gradient or sequential scale in the diagram: confirm it uses a single-hue value progression (light to dark) or a diverging value scale, not a hue progression (green → yellow → red). The hue-ordered scale is a construction error. | Bertin 1967/1983, pp. 87–89; [axismaps.com](https://www.axismaps.com/guide/visual-variables) |
| 8 | **Absence-of-marks rule** | Check whether empty spaces in the diagram are intentional and correct. In a signifying field (matrix, grid), absence of a mark signifies absence of the phenomenon. If empty cells mean "not applicable" vs. "value = zero" vs. "no data," and these distinctions are not encoded, the construction contains informational lacunae. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 46 |
| 9 | **Length constraint: color** | Count the number of distinct hue categories in the diagram. If the count exceeds 7, the color variable has exceeded its discriminable length — the viewer cannot reliably distinguish all categories. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 85; [countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf) |
| 10 | **Length constraint: size** | Count the number of distinct size categories used for a data component. If the count exceeds 4 (for points) or 4 (for lines), the size variable has exceeded its discriminable length. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 96; [countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf) |
| 11 | **Overall-level test** | State the primary question the diagram is designed to answer (e.g., "Which services have critical dependencies?"). Cover the diagram's legend. Measure the time required to answer the question. If > ~3 seconds for a trained viewer, the diagram is functioning as a figuration, not an image. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 139; [Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jacques_Bertin) |
| 12 | **Icon/symbol separation** | For each icon or pictogram in the diagram: determine whether its shape is encoding the invariant (entity type — constant, not a variable) or a data component (something that varies). If shape is encoding a data component, apply the shape audit (Test #4). | Bertin 1967/1983, pp. 95–96, 50–51 |
| 13 | **Line variable inventory** | For each edge/line in a network or flow diagram, list all retinal variables active on that line (weight, value, color, dashing). Count the total. If >2 active retinal variables on lines, test whether the overall question remains answerable in a single glance. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 96; [countersubject.biz PDF](https://countersubject.biz/wp-content/uploads/2024/08/Semiology-of-Graphics-Diagrams-Networks-Maps-Jacques-Bertin-Z-Library.pdf) |
| 14 | **QS² error check** | Identify any diagram where a size- or value-encoded quantity is displayed inside or over a zone (area region) that is *itself* scaled by a different quantity. Verify the viewer cannot misread the product of the two quantities as the encoded value. | Bertin 1967/1983, p. 45 |
| 15 | **Network complexity threshold** | Count the number of nodes and edges in any network diagram. Apply Bertin's qualitative rule: when element count makes the figure "complex, illegible, and untransformable," the network has exceeded its capacity to function as a graphic and requires decomposition or a matrix alternative. | Bertin, *La Graphique*, 1981, p. 129; [Wikiquote](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Jacques_Bertin) |

## Appendix B: Bertin vs. Tufte — Comparative Framework

| Principle | Bertin's Formulation | Tufte's Formulation |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose of graphics** | To answer a question at the overall level in a single instant of visual perception; the graphic is a processing instrument, not only a representation. | To give the viewer the greatest number of ideas in the shortest time, with the least ink, in the smallest space. |
| **Theory type** | *A priori* construction rules: specify the correct encoding *before* building the graphic. | *Post-hoc* evaluative heuristics: judge an existing graphic against aesthetic and ethical principles. |
| **Falsifiability** | Fully falsifiable: a specific variable assignment either matches or violates the data type. No subjective threshold. | Partially falsifiable: data-ink ratio is defined but requires judgment to calculate; "chartjunk" requires aesthetic interpretation. |
| **Core unit of analysis** | The visual variable and its assignment to a data component. | The ink on the page and its ratio to information conveyed. |
| **Color** | Hue is nominal only. Value (lightness) is ordered. Using hue for ordered data is a construction error by formal definition. | Multiple cases where color is misused, addressed rhetorically. No formal rule distinguishing hue from value. |
| **Multi-variable encoding** | Three-component maximum for a single image. Beyond three, the construction is a figuration requiring sequential reading. | "The number of information-carrying dimensions depicted should not exceed the number of dimensions in the data" (Principle 6). Agrees in principle; does not specify a count. |
| **Minimalism** | Not a design goal per se. The correct number of variables is the number needed to encode the data components at their correct levels. | "Maximize the data-ink ratio." Non-data-ink should be erased "within reason." |
| **Small multiples** | Implicit in the "set of images" approach — multiple panels, each answering one question at the overall level. | Explicitly advocated as a major visualization strategy: "above all else, show comparisons." |
| **Ordered data** | Must use size, value, or texture — variables with the ordered perceptual property. Any other choice is a level mismatch. | Show order using position or length (Cleveland-influenced preference); warns against "lying about the data" through misordering. |
| **Chart types** | Not categorized as good/bad; assessed by whether the construction matches the data's components and levels. | Specific types condemned (pie charts, 3D charts) or praised (scatter plots, small multiples) by case study. |
| **Quantitative data** | Requires planar dimensions or size (the only quantitative variables). Approximation is inherent: "accurate approximation but not a precise measurement." | Requires proportional representation: "the representation of numbers should be directly proportional to numerical quantities." |
| **Area of agreement** | Both condemn: hue-encoded ordered data, decorative variation, and multiple pie charts. Both endorse: positional encoding for quantitative data. | — |
| **Area of genuine disagreement** | Bertin: matching variable to data type is the primary criterion. Tufte: minimizing non-data-ink is the primary criterion. A well-matched but ink-heavy graphic is correct by Bertin; questionable by Tufte. A minimal but mismatched graphic is wrong by Bertin; potentially acceptable by Tufte. | — |
| **Scope** | Restricted to monosemic graphics (diagrams, networks, maps). Explicitly excludes figurative imagery and polysemic systems. | Broad: applies to any quantitative graphic, with case studies ranging from weather maps to stock charts to scientific figures. |
| **Historical layer** | Foundational theory — establishes the conceptual vocabulary for all subsequent frameworks (Mackinlay, Wilkinson, Vega-Lite). | Canonical criticism — establishes the evaluative vocabulary for design culture (data-ink, chartjunk, lie factor). |
