Chart toolkit · for anyone making charts at Perplexity

Charts that don't embarrass us when we ship them.

A small set of charts and diagrams, designed against Tufte and Bertin, with the spacing audit baked in. Use them. Remix them. If something's broken, the repo takes issues.

01 · Why this exists

Most chart mistakes are repeatable. Most are also invisible to the person making them.

We ship a lot of charts. Blog posts, social cards, decks, launch pages. Until last week the chart on each one was a one-off built from scratch, which meant whoever made it had to re-decide every visual question from zero. Some of those decisions went well. Many didn't. Hue-coded sequential data, color carrying both category and intensity, four retinal variables stacked on one diagram, spacing that wandered between five and eighteen pixels with no consistent reason.

None of this is malicious. The rules that catch it live in books most of us haven't read. So the goal of this page isn't to teach you Tufte or Bertin. It's to take what's in their books, what we've learned shipping real work, and turn it into defaults you can grab without becoming a data-viz expert.

Three charts, six diagrams, two audit frameworks. That's the system today. It's not finished. But it's enough to keep us from shipping the same five mistakes over and over.

02 · What bad looks like

You can't avoid mistakes you can't name. So here are the ones we've actually made.

Each pair below is a real chart we shipped (or almost shipped) and the corrected version. The point isn't to dunk on the original. The point is that the failure modes are structural, not aesthetic. Once you see them, you can't unsee them.

Before · shipped on launch day

The original. Five steps cluttered into a single horizontal band. No clear hierarchy between the action (scanning) and the outcome (the block). Bertin: 4 retinal variables active at once (color, size, position, and outline weight), which exceeds the image limit of 3.

After · same data, rebuilt

The rebuild. Position carries the flow (left to right is time). Value (border weight) separates the live step from the rest. One accent (teal) marks the punchline. That's three retinal variables. The story reads in one glance.

Before · ordered data encoded with hue

The audit harness found ten places where the spacing was below the 20px floor. That's not a taste issue. It's a math issue. Margins collapsed, sub-floor gaps fought for the same eight pixels, the chart read as cramped without anyone being able to point to why.

Same chart family, same audit

Seven violations on the frontier chart. Same root cause. A chain of margin-top and margin-bottom declarations across the section stack means the actual gap between any two elements is the result of margin collapse, which is unreliable across nested containers, browsers, and zoom levels.

The fix in both cases is the same. Stop using margins to space sections. Use a display: grid container with a single row-gap token. The gap is the contract. There's nothing to compute or collapse.

03 · The four rules

If you only remember four things, remember these.

Pulled from Bertin's Semiology of Graphics and Tufte's Visual Display of Quantitative Information. We read both books so you don't have to. The full research briefs are linked at the bottom of each rule if you want the source.

Rule 01

One accent. Pick the punchline.

Every chart has one thing the reader should take away. Color that. Leave everything else neutral. If three bars are teal, two are magenta, and one is indigo, you've encoded nothing. You've just made noise. Reserve hue for the moment that earns it.

Bertin pp. 85–91 · selective vs associative variables

Rule 02

Length on a common scale wins.

When comparing quantities, position-on-aligned-scale (bar length, point position on a shared axis) is more accurate than every other encoding by a wide margin. Cleveland and McGill ranked it #1 across forty years of perception experiments. Use bars or aligned dot plots. Resist the urge to reach for pie, area, or angle.

Cleveland & McGill 1984 · perceptual accuracy ranking

Rule 03

Hue is for categories. Value is for order.

If your data has an order (low to high, before to after, worst to best), encode that with value: a single-hue ramp from light to dark. Multiple hues to encode ordered data is a construction error. The reader has to learn a color-to-number mapping that the chart is hiding from them. It's the most common chart mistake at Perplexity, and the one fixed on the snippet bar last week.

Bertin pp. 85–91 · the construction error

Rule 04

The channel between elements is the most important spacing decision.

Whether it's a wire between two nodes, a gap between bars, or padding around a chart's data area, the channel is what makes the relationship legible. Pick one token from the spacing scale and use it everywhere. Don't let any channel collapse below 20 pixels. The system below catches you when you do.

Tufte chapter 5 · data-ink ratio and the small-multiples grid

04 · Start from these

Three chart starting points. Take any of them, swap your data in, ship.

Each one has been audited end to end. The HTML is single-file, no build step. Copy it into your blog post or deck export, edit the data object near the bottom of the file, you're done. If your chart doesn't fit any of these patterns, that's a real signal. The system grows from those gaps.

Snippet bar

Use it when you have a handful of named categories and a single quantity per category, and the story is the spread between them. Negative and positive on the same axis works fine. The bar with the punchline gets the accent; everything else goes neutral.

Frontier line

Use it when you're showing a tradeoff curve. Two continuous axes, two to four lines, and the story is which line sits higher and to the left. The hero variant gets a solid line; reference baselines go dashed in warm grey. Markers stay sparse so the curve reads as a curve.

Open live View source

Ranked scorecard

Use it when you have a leaderboard. Small set of named entities (six to ten), multiple comparable metrics, and the reader's job is to scan the ranking. The top row gets the accent; the reference row uses warm grey; the trailing row gets magenta. The accuracy bar is length-on-common-scale, which is what's doing the actual perceptual work.

05 · The audit catches you

There's a script that measures every gap in your chart and tells you if any of them are below floor.

It's called measure.cjs. It opens your HTML in a headless browser, reads the computed styles and bounding rectangles of every element, and classifies every gap against the spacing scale. Anything below 20 pixels (the --s-4 floor) is flagged as a violation. Anything between scale tokens is flagged as off-scale. Everything else passes.

Here's what the verdict looks like on the three charts above. Each ran through the harness with zero sub-floor violations. The numbers are real measurements, not claims.

Chart On scale Off scale Sub-floor Intra-label
Snippet bar 13 0 0 —
Frontier line 12 0 0 —
Ranked scorecard 20 1 0 2

The scorecard's one off-scale value is the title-text-end to mark-start gap (44 pixels), which is content-driven whitespace, not a layout decision. The two intra-label gaps are the model-name to org-label stack and the delta-arrow to delta-number gap. Both are inside a single visual unit where tight stacking is correct. The harness tracks these separately so the verdict stays honest.

If you're remixing one of the templates and you change a margin, run the harness before you ship. It catches mistakes you wouldn't catch by eye.

06 · How to use this

Three paths, depending on how much customization you need.

Path A

Copy a template, swap your data.

Open one of the three live links in section 04. Save the HTML. Edit the data array near the bottom of the file. Open the file in a browser, screenshot it, embed it in your post. No build step.

Path B

Embed the live HTML directly.

If your blog supports raw HTML embeds, drop the chart in. Fonts and colors are self-contained. The frame fits a 1316-pixel column at full resolution and scales down responsively below 880 pixels.

Path C

Request a new pattern.

If your data doesn't fit any of the three existing patterns, that's information. It means there's a real chart type missing from the system. Open an issue describing what you're trying to show. The system grows when it hits a gap.
